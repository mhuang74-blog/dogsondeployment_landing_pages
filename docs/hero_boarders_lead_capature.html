Want to Foster a Pet for a Military Service Member?

Join our interest list to learn how you can help—no commitment required.

Volunteering as a Boarder with Dogs on Deployment offers pet lovers a meaningful way to support military members, veterans, and first responders by providing temporary, loving homes for their pets during service commitments or hardships. As a Boarder, you open your home to care for dogs or cats, ensuring they are safe and cherished while their owners are deployed, training, or facing other challenges. This role involves registering on the Dogs on Deployment platform, creating a profile to showcase your pet care experience, and matching with service members whose pets’ needs align with your home environment and lifestyle. By fostering these pets, you help prevent them from being surrendered to shelters, offering peace of mind to America’s heroes and forming heartfelt connections with their furry companions.

Volunteer Spotlights

Meet some of our amazing volunteers who have made a difference in the lives of military service members and their pets.

Melanie

Devoted DoD Boarder

Melanie has been a dedicated Dogs on Deployment boarder for years, providing loving care to military pets during their owners' deployments. Her commitment to supporting military families through pet boarding has made a significant impact on the lives of service members and their beloved companions.

"Just knowing that I'm helping to relieve a service member's stress while they are deployed is gratifying. The service members are so grateful for my willingness to open my home to their pet that they almost cry with relief."

Read Melanie's Story

Audrey

From 100 Rescues to DoD Boarding

After fostering over 100 rescue animals, Audrey found a new calling through Dogs on Deployment. Her experience with rescue animals provided the perfect foundation for boarding military pets, offering both the pets and their owners peace of mind during deployments.

"With Dogs on Deployment, I feel like it's a much easier process because these animals are already living in homes, which means they should already have most basic manners. You can talk to their owner and get a sense of their personality, especially if you have other animals in your home, and you want them to be able to interact. Well, you know you don't have any of those options when you're just fostering for a rescue or a shelter, because you don't have any history on the animal most of the time. So it's just a much easier process."
